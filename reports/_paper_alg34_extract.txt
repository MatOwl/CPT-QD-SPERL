C.2.3. Filtering Through our theoretical analyses in Section 3, we have seen how CPT prediction errors may
translate to SPE learning performance, where we can manage our errors (make them as small as possible) by increasing
𝐾. Algorithm 2 is a sample-based (approximate) version of QDRL with QR and SA playing significant roles in its
update construction. Through this section, we propose a filtering method as another subroutine in Algorithm 4 to
address two scenarios that interfere with CPT predictions in Algorithm 2.
Discrete return distributions. One requirement for QR is a continuous target variable since otherwise, the
quantile function becomes not bijective. However, in our context, this target variable is the return distributions and
there are many environments (including ours) that induce discrete return distributions. Despite we can still run the
QR-based update in Algorithm 2, Line 17 as it is, our experiments suggest that given a discrete target distribution, the
quantile estimates converge to the quantiles of a “smoothened” version of the target. This introduces irreducible errors
that will interfere with SPE learning when the error magnitude is larger than the corresponding action gap. Algorithm
4 addresses this issue by filtering out the “smoothened” parts (𝑑 𝑗 > 𝑑 ∗ in Line 6) of the distribution estimate output by
Algorithm 2 and sharpening the entire quantiles (Lines 11-15) to emulate discrete distributions better; see Figure 11
for illustration. The hyperparameter 𝑓 𝑖𝑙𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑇𝑟𝑒𝑠ℎ controls how many quantiles are filtered out, while the𝑡𝑟𝑒𝑠ℎ𝑅𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜 is
used (Lines 20-22) to prevent the filter from throwing away too many quantiles such that the filtered distribution itself
does not reflect the QR estimates; for instance, when a discrete target distribution has more values in its support, the
same filterTresh may cut out some informative parts believing that they are “smoothened”.
Crossing quantiles. Another issue that can be commonly found in quantile approximation is crossing quantiles,
where the quantile estimates 𝜑𝑘 are not monotonically increasing in 𝑘. In our context, this issue mainly arises from the
SA step with finite samples 𝑁. We can expect such noisy and crossing quantiles to interfere with CPT prediction that
makes use of all quantile values and locations, with CPT weights attached to each. Algorithm 4 alleviates this issue by
filtering out the quantiles that are less than its preceding quantile ( 𝑑 𝑗 < 0 in Lines 5-6). The output of Algorithm 4 is
applied to replace the quantile estimates𝜑𝑖+1

The first source of SPE learning error comes from the states with very small action gaps. Here, even small prediction

ties by the algorithm due to erroneous Q-function estimates; intuitively, for a small action gap Δ, we have Δ-ties that
are induced by errors larger than Δ.
